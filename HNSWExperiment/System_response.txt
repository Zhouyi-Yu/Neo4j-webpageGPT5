Why this is helpful to reinforcement learning (RL)

Provides accurate, high-dimensional state representations: A validated automated 3D reconstruction produces reliable 3D spine geometry and clinical measurements (angles, vertebral poses). That dense, structured state is ideal for RL agents that must learn control or decision policies that depend on patient anatomy (e.g., image-guided robot control, simulated surgical planning).
Enables realistic simulation environments: The reconstruction pipeline can be used to generate patient-specific 3D models for simulators. RL benefits from realistic simulators (model-based RL, domain randomization, sim-to-real transfer) and needs many episodes — automated reconstruction scales generation of realistic patient models.
Reduces annotation cost for training and evaluation: Automated methods produce labels (vertebral positions, angles) at scale, which can be used as ground-truth or weak labels for RL reward design, imitation learning, or offline RL datasets without costly manual annotation.
Supports reward shaping and safety constraints: Quantitative clinical measurements (e.g., Cobb angle, vertebral rotation) can be transformed into reward functions or safety constraints (penalize unsafe anatomy configurations), making RL policies clinically interpretable and constrained.
Facilitates sample efficiency and transfer learning: With accurate 3D reconstructions, one can pretrain perception modules (state encoders) or learn dynamics models that speed up RL convergence and improve transfer from simulation to real-world interventions.
Enables evaluation and benchmarking: Validated reconstruction and measurement give objective metrics for comparing RL policies (e.g., how much a candidate intervention reduces measured curvature), aiding reproducible evaluation.
